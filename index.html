Welcome to my webpage. I am a post-doctoral researcher with the USDA Forest Service in the PNW Research Station located in Corvallis.
My current research examines the spatial distribution of forest structure and wildland fuels in eastern Washington, western Oregon and the central Sierra Nevada using Gradient Nearest Neighbor imputation. Forest management requires detailed spatial information over very large regions, however the resources do not exist to effectively sample such regions. Because of this, researchers and managers rely on sampling plus remote sensing from satellites to provide information that can't be obtained first hand. While this is often referred to as mapping it differs from traditonal mapping in that maps are usually drawn from know data or traced from photographs. Our forest structure "maps" are the results of statistical models. As such, much of my research deals with understanding the spatial accuracy of these models and trying to improve them.
My dissertation research examined woody regeneration patterns in montane systems at multiple scales. I am interested in how the physical template imposes limitations upon species distributions and how climatic changes might alter the physical template causing a shift in species patterns. My research also deals with the scale-specific phenomena of pattern detection in nature and the abilitiy of sampling designs to capture them
